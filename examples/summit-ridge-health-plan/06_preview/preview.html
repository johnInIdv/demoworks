Summit Ridge Health Plan: every reviewer, the same read of the same file

Prepared for Karen Whitfield, Director of Utilization Review · ahead of our demo call

You run ~3,200 prior-auth requests a month with twelve reviewers. The complex files — spine, inpatient, specialty pharmacy — arrive as 100–150-page packets, and a reviewer spends the first hour just finding what's in them. You have written medical-necessity criteria, yet two reviewers reach different determinations on roughly one in six complex cases. Below is what we heard, and what we'd show you — on your own kind of case, not a generic deck.

What we heard

The variance isn't usually about medicine. It's about what each reviewer found in the file — and that variance is what drives your six-week, ~180-case appeals backlog, some of it on statutory clocks.

"One reviewer found the conservative-therapy documentation on page ninety and the other one missed it. It wasn't a disagreement about medicine. It was a disagreement about what was in the file." — Dr. Elias Hale

What we'd show you

1. A medical-necessity checklist that gives every reviewer the same structured read

Lead · Pattern 6Custom build

We configure an Atlas Skill against your written spine criteria. It takes a claim file and goes criterion by criterion — met, not met, or not documented — each with a citation back to the page. Run on a de-identified spine packet you provide, every reviewer starts from the same cited read, instead of each hunting through 140 pages alone.

Sample output — synthetic L4–L5 lumbar-fusion packet (131 pp., consolidated)

The one flagged item is exactly your page-90 problem: the conservative-therapy evidence exists but is scattered across pages 14, 41–44, and 90 — and page 43 is an illegible handwritten note. The checklist surfaces it as not documented with the citations, so the reviewer confirms or refutes in one click instead of silently missing it.
Honest note: the technology isn't the hard part — getting your "ugly" spine policy into clean, structured criteria is. That conversion runs through the Determination Library and needs a clinical owner to curate it (Dr. Hale volunteered — that's the right instinct).

2. A one-page reviewer brief of the whole file, with citations

Supporting · Pattern 3Out of the box

Load the consolidated claim file into Caseboard and ask across it: documented onset, what conservative care was tried and for how long, what imaging showed. The hour of orientation becomes a cited one-page brief in seconds.

Honest note: Caseboard is single-user today — every reviewer gets this on their own files, not one shared team board (shared boards are on the roadmap, not shipped). Retrieval is best on a consolidated text file, and OCR on handwriting is uneven.

3. "How did we decide this kind of case before?"

Next phase · Pattern 4Out of the box

Caseboard can compare a new submission against a prior file and surface what's consistent and what diverges — a starting answer to the precedent question you said you can never answer fast.

Honest note: same single-user gate — there is no shared, team-wide precedent board yet. The canonical "what's our position on this criterion" answer is what the Determination Library is for, once it's built. We won't sell you a precedent board that doesn't exist.

What this is — and isn't

  • It is an analytical aid that gives your reviewers a consistent, cited starting point and shows its work against the record.
  • It is not a coverage determination. Your reviewers make the call; nothing we produce becomes the determination of record. We'll say that again with legal in the room.
